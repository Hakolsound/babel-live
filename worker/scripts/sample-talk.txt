The core challenge we're addressing is diagnostic accuracy at scale. When a radiologist examines an MRI scan, they bring decades of experience to the table.
But what happens when that radiologist is fatigued, or when they're working in a low-resource setting without access to experienced colleagues?
This is exactly where deep learning becomes valuable. We've built a system called MedVision that can analyze these scans with accuracy comparable to a board-certified radiologist.
Now I want to be very clear about what this system is, and what it isn't. It is not a replacement for human judgment.
Think of it as a second pair of eyes — a very fast, very consistent second pair of eyes that never gets tired and never has a bad day.
The model was trained on 1.2 million annotated scans collected from 47 hospitals across three continents.
That geographic and equipment diversity in the training data is absolutely critical. A model trained only on data from a single institution tends to latch on to that hospital's imaging artifacts rather than the true pathological features we care about.
We call this failure mode "shortcut learning," and it's one of the most dangerous problems in medical AI today.
In our independent validation cohort, MedVision detected early-stage glioblastoma with 94% sensitivity and 91% specificity.
For context, the human radiologist baseline in that same cohort was 89% sensitivity and 93% specificity.
So our system outperforms on sensitivity but is slightly behind on specificity. What does that tradeoff mean in a real clinical setting?
It means we catch more cases, but we also flag more false positives that require follow-up. For a first-pass screening tool, that is an acceptable and even desirable tradeoff.
Looking ahead, we plan to integrate MedVision into the clinical workflow at three partner hospitals starting in the third quarter of this year.
The integration is intentionally non-disruptive — it surfaces as a soft alert inside the PACS system that radiologists already use every day.
We're not asking anyone to change their workflow. We're just adding a quiet, reliable colleague to the reading room.
